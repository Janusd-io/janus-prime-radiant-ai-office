# Anti-AI-washing as content discipline — "name three things that make it AI"

## What we learned

A working content / positioning discipline emerged from the 12 May Andrew session, originating in a real-world example Michael spotted on his way to the meeting: a property development branded *"AI Powered Living."* Andrew's instinctive reaction crystallised the discipline:

> "That should be the default question. Whenever someone says AI thing, AI powered or AI named or whatever, I want to say what are the three things that make that AI powered? And that's going to be my automatic response. And they're going to tell me, we built a slide deck using ChatGPT. That's not AI."

The frame: forcing claims about "AI" to be backed by *three concrete capabilities* filters out cosmetic AI-washing and leaves only substance.

## Why this matters to Janus

Three reasons, in order of immediacy:

1. **Differentiator for Janus's [[ai-native-janus-positioning|AI Native pitch]].** Janus *is* doing the substantive thing (Prime Radiant, Marketing PR build, ISO compliance, agentic workflows) while a large fraction of "AI powered" branding in the market is cosmetic. The test gives Janus an automatic way to position above the noise.
2. **Internal discipline against our own AI-washing.** The same test applies to Janus content. Anything we publish that claims "AI" should pass our own three-things test. Andrew committed to using the test as a default question; same standard applies to outbound copy.
3. **Tone signal for the brand.** Light-touch, fun, slightly jokey — exactly the tone Andrew wants the Janus social presence to carry. The lesson and the tactic are inseparable: this is positioning *and* voice.

## The first instance

Andrew committed to posting today (12 May) on the AI-washing example Michael shared. Pattern proposed: a series of posts surfacing real-world examples of AI-washing in the wild, asking the question, sharing the results. Doubles as engagement-bait and as a values statement.

> Andrew: "We can do a series of posts of what's the most ridiculous AI powered thing you see, and that can even start engaging people. That's the sort of thing that we need to do and create some fun around our mission, but in a light hearted way."

## Operational guidance

When evaluating an "AI"-claimed Janus output (campaign, white paper, landing page, deck, product feature):

1. Can the team name *three specific capabilities* that the AI is doing in the artefact?
2. If yes — the claim stands.
3. If no — strip the "AI" framing or substantiate it. Default to substance over signalling.

When evaluating external "AI"-claimed work:

1. Apply the same test publicly.
2. When the answer is "we built a slide deck using ChatGPT" or similar, that's content fodder, not a competitor to take seriously.

## Why this is non-obvious

The AI-washing tide is so high in mid-2026 that most companies have stopped questioning it. The instinct is to ride the wave — add "AI powered" to product copy because it's expected. The discipline reverses the default: if you're claiming AI, you should be able to back it. The audience asking the question is the brand audience.
