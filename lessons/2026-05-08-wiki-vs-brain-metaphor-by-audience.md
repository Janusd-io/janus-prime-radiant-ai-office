# "Wiki" vs "brain" — the supporting metaphor needs to match the audience

## What happened

In the 2026-05-08 brainstorm with [[andrew-soane]] (CMO), Michael was demoing the AIO Prime Radiant. Andrew, after seeing the synthesis layer (briefs, derived insights, knowledge graph, pulse), reached for a marker and started sketching a Marketing-domain instance. He labelled the diagram "marketing wiki" — and immediately corrected himself:

> "There's a reason why that's so small actually. We are marking the brain... it's a brain and it's not just [a wiki]."

Michael's response: *"That's exactly. It's a brain, it's not a wiki. That's what I'm calling the Prime Radiant."*

Andrew confirmed: *"You have a better name for [it]? Wiki didn't do it for me because it's not a wiki, it's a brain."*

## The lesson

**The system's proper name is fixed (Janus Prime Radiant). The supporting metaphor that helps any given audience grasp it is not.**

For technical audiences (engineers, AI Office), "wiki" is fine — it accurately describes the surface form (markdown files, frontmatter, wikilinks, knowledge graph). For non-technical leadership audiences, "wiki" pattern-matches to *static* (Wikipedia, Confluence, internal documentation graveyards) and undersells the synthesis / inference / pattern-spotting capability that makes the system worth building. "Brain" works because it conveys:
- Active thought, not passive storage
- Pattern recognition, not just retrieval
- Memory + synthesis, not just records
- Something that grows and connects, not something that's filed and forgotten

The Karpathy origin uses "LLM Wiki" because Karpathy is talking to engineers about a personal-knowledge-management pattern. Janus is selling the *value* of the pattern to operators, executives, and department heads — different audience, different supporting metaphor.

## How to apply

- **System name stays:** Janus Prime Radiant (proper noun; Asimov-grounded; signals gravity).
- **Pitch metaphor varies:** "company brain" / "department brain" / "your brain" for non-technical audiences; "LLM wiki" / "knowledge graph" / "institutional KB" for technical contexts.
- **In wiki content** (briefs, project hubs, lessons, this very page): use "Janus Prime Radiant" as the canonical term, but allow "brain" as the supporting metaphor in pitches, demos, and external comms.
- **The concept page** [[llm-wiki]] keeps its name — it documents Karpathy's *methodology* and is correctly labelled for the engineering audience that originated it. Janus Prime Radiant is one *implementation* of that methodology.

## Cross-cutting application

This lesson generalises: every Janus-internal product or system that's pitched to multiple audiences has the same problem. The proper noun is fixed; the metaphor is a localisation. Watch for it in CRM rollout (CRM ≠ "system of record" depending on who you're talking to), in marketing-tech (campaign automation ≠ "drip workflow"), and in compliance (ISO 9001 ≠ "the audit thing").

## Why it matters

Naming friction in early demos can kill department adoption before the *thing being demoed* gets a fair hearing. Andrew was already converted by the time he hit the wiki/brain naming friction — he was sketching a Marketing instance on the screen. But if the metaphor mismatch had hit earlier (in the first 5 minutes of the demo), it could have cost him the willingness to engage. For the Marketing pilot, HR pilot, and every subsequent rollout: lead with "department brain," circle back to "wiki" only with audiences who'd find it more legible than the metaphor.
