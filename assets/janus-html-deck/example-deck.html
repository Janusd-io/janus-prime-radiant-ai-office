Quarterly planning approach

Better cadence, clearer priorities.

How a small team plans, prioritises, and ships within a twelve-week quarter without losing focus.

Three constraints shape how we plan

Every team is working under conditions that rule out most institutional planning approaches. Ours are no different.

Team capacity

Small team distributed across product, design, and engineering. Hiring through the quarter is limited; the plan needs to fit the people we have.

Quarter length

Twelve calendar weeks. Holidays and review cycles land in the middle. Real execution time is closer to nine weeks.

Cross-team dependencies

Several initiatives need input from teams we don't control directly. Dependencies need to be surfaced early or they become blockers late.

The question isn't "what's the best plan?" — it's "what's the best plan we can actually execute?"

Two shifts have changed how planning works

Both have moved the trade-off between planning rigor and execution time.

Tools matured

Modern project trackers ship with built-in summaries, dependency views, and automation. The planning artefact and the execution surface are now the same thing — the doc updates itself.

Last quarter we cut planning-meeting time by roughly 60% using these tools to drive the agenda.

Async became the default

Written proposals get decided faster than live workshops. The team has shifted to "write a one-pager, comment async, decide in 48 hours" for any meaningful decision.

Q2 retro flagged that roughly 80% of major decisions happened async, in writing.

Together, these collapse the overhead of running a quarter — provided the planning approach is selected on this basis.

Two viable planning approaches

Same outcomes — shipped work, team alignment, stakeholder confidence — achievable two different ways.

Path A

Waterfall quarterly planning

Big planning kickoff. Full-quarter scope locked. Weekly status meetings. Heavy review cycles. Re-plan if anything changes.

Predictability: High when scope holds.
Overhead: High. Multiple weeks of every quarter spent in planning meetings.
Right for: Large teams with stable scope and deep dependencies.
Path B · Recommended

Lean iterative planning

Three-to-five quarterly outcomes. Weekly async updates. Mid-quarter review. Light scope, frequent adjustment, no surprise re-plans.

Predictability: High for the outcomes, flexible on the means.
Overhead: Low. Planning is the work, not separate from it.
Right for: Small teams with autonomy and changing context.

Path A isn't wrong — it's optimised for a team shape we don't have.

The math behind the approach

9
weeks of actual execution time

left after holidays, planning, and review cycles are subtracted from the twelve-week quarter.

The implication

Heavy upfront planning eats into the only nine weeks the team has to ship anything.

Two weeks spent on quarterly planning is two weeks out of nine. That's 22% of the team's quarter spent producing a plan rather than executing one.

The lean approach reaches the same outcomes by treating planning as a continuous activity rather than a discrete phase.

A framework for prioritising work

Every candidate initiative gets evaluated against three questions before it enters the quarterly outcomes list.

1

Impact

If we ship this, what changes for our users or the business?

Why it matters

Filters out work that's interesting but doesn't move the metrics that matter.

2

Effort

How much of the team's nine-week execution window does this consume?

Why it matters

Anchors the conversation to a real capacity budget, not an aspirational one.

3

Confidence

How sure are we this will work, and what would we need to learn to be more sure?

Why it matters

Surfaces risky-but-high-impact bets explicitly rather than burying them.

High on all three = clear yes. High on two = sequence carefully. High on one = usually defer.

The planning artefacts

Each artefact has a specific purpose and a clear owner. Nothing exists "just to have it".

Artefact Owner Purpose
Quarterly outcomes doc Team lead Three to five outcomes for the quarter. Written once at the start, reviewed mid-quarter, retrospected at the end.
Weekly async update Each contributor What shipped, what's stuck, what changed. Posted in shared channel by Friday. Replaces the Monday status meeting.
Decision log Whoever raised the decision One-pager for any decision affecting more than one person. Comment async, decide in 48 hours.
Mid-quarter review Team lead Two-hour session at end of week 6. Are we still aimed at the right outcomes? Adjust scope, not goals.
Retrospective Rotating facilitator End of quarter. What worked, what didn't, what changes for next quarter. Output feeds the next outcomes doc.

Total planning overhead: ~2 hours/week per person, distributed across writing updates, reviewing decisions, and the mid-quarter review.

Quarter cadence

Twelve weeks broken into four phases. The middle two are the bulk of execution; the outer two handle planning and learning.

1
Weeks 1–2
Set outcomes

Outcomes doc drafted, reviewed async, finalised by end of week 2. Three to five outcomes max.

2
Weeks 3–6
Sprint 1

First execution block. Weekly async updates. Mid-quarter review at end of week 6 — adjust scope based on what was learned.

3
Weeks 7–10
Sprint 2

Second execution block. Apply learnings from sprint 1. Tighten focus on the outcomes that still matter.

4
Weeks 11–12
Review & reset

Retrospective, outcome scoring, next-quarter outcomes draft. Outputs feed phase 1 of the next quarter.

The pattern repeats every quarter: minimal planning ceremony, continuous adjustment, explicit handoff to the next quarter via the retrospective output.

What this changes day-to-day

The approach isn't a constraint on what gets done — it's a structure that gives the team more time for the actual work.

For contributors: less time in meetings

Async updates replace status meetings. Decisions move to written one-pagers. The Monday and Friday calendar should feel noticeably lighter — that's the point.

For team leads: clearer signal from updates

Written weekly updates surface trends that status meetings tend to mask. Trouble shows up earlier; quiet wins show up at all.

For cross-team partners: visible priorities

The quarterly outcomes doc is public to anyone who needs to plan around us. Dependencies surface early; surprises drop.

The aim is to spend more of the quarter doing the work, less of it planning the work.

What we're asking for

Three commitments worth being explicit about, so we can run the next quarter on this basis and evaluate honestly at the end.

1

Try the lean approach for one quarter.

Q3 2026. Full commitment to the cadence — async updates, written decisions, mid-quarter review, retrospective.

2

Measure the outcomes, not the artefacts.

At end of quarter, judge by what shipped and team health — not by how thick the planning doc was.

3

Adjust at the retro, not in the middle.

Mid-quarter changes scope, not approach. Approach adjustments happen at the end of the quarter, based on the retro output.
