# Broken Ladder paper — WFH confounds the GenAI-junior-decline story

Lambert & Schindler (LSE/Warwick/EIT) working paper, May 2026: the post-2022 decline in early-career hiring is robustly predicted by WFH exposure, NOT GenAI exposure. When estimated jointly across 243M new hires and 407M job postings (US/UK/CA/AU 2017-2025), the GenAI coefficient attenuates to statistical insignificance once WFH is controlled for.

## Why this matters

A methodological counter-weight to the dominant narrative that GenAI is hollowing out junior-hire pipelines — the narrative is everywhere right now (see [[2026-05-25-ai-labor-restructuring-bank-and-tech-wave]] for the recent enterprise version). This paper argues the data-attribution underlying that narrative is contaminated by the parallel WFH shock.

In plain terms: yes, junior-share of hiring fell. But the mechanism may be that remote work raised the cost of supervising and training juniors (Emanuel et al. 2024 / 2026), eroding the value of investing in early-career talent — not that GenAI replaced them at their tasks.

## Implications for the AIO

- Whenever an AIO brief or external presentation invokes "GenAI is killing junior jobs", this paper is the methodological caveat. Cite it; don't propagate the un-caveated claim.
- Connects to [[observed-exposure-ai-labor-measure]] — both research threads are working on the same first-order question (how to measure GenAI's actual labor-market effect) with different approaches.
- For Janus client conversations: when leaders cite junior-displacement as the AI risk, the empirical story is more contested than headlines suggest. Useful nuance for credibility.

## Watch for

- Responses from Brynjolfsson et al. and the existing GenAI-displacement literature.
- Replications / contradictions on other datasets (especially non-Anglophone markets).
- Whether the paper's framework gets folded into the broader [[observed-exposure-ai-labor-measure]] methodology debate.
