# Pilot in Command

Janus Digital's accountability framing for AI use, codified in §5.9 of the [[ai-policy|AI Policy v2.1]]: **AI is a tool; the employee remains the Pilot in Command.** All staff are professionally accountable for work produced under their name, regardless of AI assistance.

> "Employees must verify facts, validate logic, and critically assess AI outputs before use or distribution."
> — Section 5.9, Accountability & Transparency

## The principle

Aviation borrowed the term. The Pilot in Command of an aircraft is the final authority — not the autopilot, not the flight management system, not the ground crew. The PIC delegates routine work to subsystems but retains ultimate responsibility for the safe conduct of the flight. The PIC cannot evade accountability by blaming a subsystem failure.

Applied to AI at Janus: the employee using a Claude / Cowork / Claude Code session is the PIC. Outputs produced under that employee's name carry the employee's professional accountability — not the model's, not the AI Office's, not the vendor's. The AI is a subsystem; the employee delegates routine work to it but retains the authority to verify, override, and decline its output.

## Operating consequences

Three rules follow directly from the principle:

1. **Verify before use or distribution.** Routine hallucinations are corrected by the user as part of their standard professional responsibilities; only extreme deviation, recurring systemic failures, or guardrail breaches escalate to the AI Office (§5.8 Incident Reporting). The implicit expectation: every employee using AI tools knows the failure modes of the tools they use and verifies accordingly.
2. **No "AI Slop."** Raw, unverified, low-quality AI-generated content is **prohibited from distribution** (§5.9). Distributing AI output without verification is a policy violation distinct from an honest mistake; the prohibition exists to prevent the Pilot in Command from delegating accountability *back* to the AI.
3. **Transparency disclosure.** Internal and external communications that are *significantly* generated by AI must be disclosed (§5.9). The PIC remains accountable for the substance even when disclosing AI generation.

## Why this framing matters

Other AI governance frameworks default to "human in the loop" — a phrase the [[coordination-leverage-model|Coordination Leverage Model]] flags as vague (*"more rigorous than the vague 'human in the loop' mantra that most AI governance frameworks rely on"*). Pilot in Command is more rigorous because it locates accountability specifically: with the named employee, not a generic "human."

This has direct implications for role design and the [[builders-sellers-measurers|builders / sellers / measurers]] frame. In an AI-augmented organisation, the most valuable individual contributors are not those who produce the most output, but those who exercise the best judgment about what to produce — i.e., the contributors who take the Pilot-in-Command responsibility seriously. Volume-producing employees who delegate verification back to the AI are the failure mode the principle is designed to prevent.

## Calibration with agent autonomy

The [[coordination-leverage-model]] §5 introduces a calibration principle that pairs with Pilot in Command: **agent autonomy is calibrated per task, not per agent**. Routine, low-risk coordination tasks (transcript → structured summary → SoR update) run with minimal human oversight; high-stakes decisions require tight HITL. Pilot in Command is the *accountability* frame; per-task autonomy calibration is the *operational* frame. Both apply.

§5.8 of the policy codifies the HITL requirement: *"For high-stakes decisions, AI must serve as a decision-support tool, not a decision-maker. A qualified human must always review and approve the final output."*

## Watch for

- **Edge cases where the PIC role is ambiguous** — e.g., automated pipelines that publish output without a named human reviewer (recurring reports, dashboards, agent-to-agent communications). Each such pipeline needs an explicit PIC assignment.
- **Pilot-in-Command in cross-agent workflows.** When [[standup|/standup]] dispatches `/ai-registry` which dispatches `/ai-tool-evaluation`, the chain of accountability needs a single PIC at the entry point — typically Michael as the curator, or the named requester for tool-evaluation runs.
- **Disclosure thresholds.** The policy says "significantly generated by AI" must be disclosed. What counts as "significant" needs an operational standard — probably a percentage threshold or a use-case taxonomy.
