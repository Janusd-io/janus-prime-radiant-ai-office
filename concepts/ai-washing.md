# AI Washing

The practice of rebranding existing non-AI products, services, or processes as "AI-powered" or "AI-driven" to capitalise on hype, without meaningful underlying AI capability. Distinct from genuine AI adoption: AI washing inflates a marketing label without changing what the product actually does.

The term first appeared in Janus context at the 2026-05-12 marketing meeting with [[andrew-soane]], framed as a market backlash Janus should engage with — possibly satirically rather than defensively.

## Why it matters

For Janus positioning: many competitors are AI-washing — claiming capability they don't possess, or attaching "AI-powered" labels to ordinary automation. This creates a credibility opportunity for Janus to position by demonstrable substance rather than slogans (see [[ai-native-janus-positioning]]).

For the broader market: AI washing is now well-documented as an industry-wide PR phenomenon. The 2026-05-24 Guardian piece [[2026-05-24-guardian-ai-washing-pr-firms]] reports UK PR firms describing "yoga-level stretches" their clients demand to be pitched as AI companies — including a shoe retailer (AllBirds) pivoting to GPU acquisition, "AI-powered basketball hoops", and "AI-powered lasers" sold as personal safety devices. Journalists are reportedly tuning out: "you can almost hear the eyes roll when you mention the word AI to a reporter."

This fuels a credibility-deficit dynamic: real AI capability becomes harder to communicate because the signal is drowned in noise. Janus positioning needs to assume the audience is already saturated and skeptical.

## Adjacent dynamics

- The broader US/UK public AI backlash documented in [[2026-05-25-ai-public-backlash-strengthens-us]] — AI washing is one trigger among several.
- Editorial / journalist push-back patterns are now standardised; the Guardian article cites three different agencies independently using the same "Bikram yoga", "Kool-Aid" framings.

## Watch for

- Specific competitors using AI-washing patterns in Janus's markets — track for positioning leverage.
- Regulatory pressure on AI claims (SEC enforcement against AI-washing public companies has begun in the US) — possible analogue in UK / EU under FCA, ASA, EU AI Act.
- Whether the "AI-washing" framing itself becomes a positioning tactic Janus can deploy (saying "we don't do that" is also a claim, and a defensible one if backed by demonstrable substance).
