A second-tier port city in South Korea is emerging as the epicenter of a growing global fight over how to split the spoils of the artificial intelligence boom.

Home to a mega-factory run by [Samsung Electronics Co.](https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/005930:KS), Pyeongtaek nearly shut down this month as employees threatened a strike that risked disrupting global supply chains. The chip giant ended up offering some full-time employees in its memory division bonuses as high as $400,000 — roughly four times Samsung’s average annual salary — to defuse the crisis.

Yet that solution created a new set of problems. Under the tiered payout system, only employees in Samsung’s semiconductors division — about 60% of its Korean workforce — will get the largest payments. Workers who aren’t involved in chip production are set to get only $4,000 apiece, a mere 1% of the biggest winners in the labor fight.

The outcome enraged a sizable chunk of Samsung employees, prompting thousands to quit its biggest union during the wage negotiations. One employee, who requested anonymity to speak openly, said the gaping compensation divide had killed motivation for workers who don’t make memory chips.

Photographs of Samsung executives at a rally at the company’s semiconductor plant in Pyeongtaek, South Korea on April 23.Photographer: SeongJoon Cho/Bloomberg

Another person in Pyeongtaek, who worked at a Samsung partner firm for 20 years, described the atmosphere as tense. “That’s someone else’s world, not ours,” said the employee, when asked how it felt to see colleagues win a life-changing pile of cash. “Of course, it stings.”

The fault lines emerging within the Samsung factory in Pyeongtaek reflect a wider debate spreading across the world as companies and governments grapple with the fallout of an AI boom that is reshaping markets, economies and industries. That’s creating a new class divide between “ [AI aristocrats](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-24/ex-bundesbank-chief-weber-warns-on-ai-aristocrats-in-job-shock "AI aristocrats") ” and the general workforce, sometimes even within the same company.

South Korea is fast emerging as one of the biggest victors of the global AI race, with chip giants Samsung and SK Hynix Inc. powering past the $1 trillion valuation mark to become two of the world’s most profitable companies. Their success is forcing policymakers in Seoul to confront an era-defining problem: How can governments equitably spread the benefits of a technology that threatens to render large swathes of the workforce redundant?

That question is becoming more urgent by the day, as a drumbeat of companies around the globe announce layoffs to focus on AI. Standard Chartered Plc CEO Bill Winters’ recent remark that he plans to replace what he called “lower-value human capital” with artificial intelligence crystallized the fears of workers everywhere about being left behind.

Tech moguls such as Elon Musk have sought to counter dystopian visions of AI with talk of “amazing abundance” and “universal high income,” in part to allay fears that “universal basic income” would lead to a permanent underclass. Figuring out how to pay for it all in a world with more debt and higher interest rates is only adding to the conundrum.

While such questions remain largely theoretical in much of the world, policymakers in South Korea and Taiwan, also at the heart of the chip boom, are already trying to find solutions. In a 2,500-word viral social media musing, South Korea’s presidential policy chief Kim Yong-beom suggested using AI-driven tax revenues to fund what he called a “citizen dividend.”

“A rare historical possibility now lies before Korea,” Kim wrote on Facebook. “The possibility of becoming not only a country that supplies AI infrastructure, but the first country to return the excess profits of the AI era to human life.”

Pyeongtaek is ground zero of that debate. Located some 50 kilometers (30 miles) south of Seoul, the Samsung plant occupies an area equivalent to 400 soccer fields. Eight-lane roads wrap around the facilities while buses stream in and out of the site that is undergoing a major expansion to meet demand — the country’s chip exports [soared](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/s-korea-s-early-trade-data-reflect-sustained-semiconductor-boom "Chips") by 202% in the first 20 days of May from a year earlier.

“It’s basically the rich getting richer and the poor getting poorer,” said Hong Seong-pil, a taxi driver who’s lived in Pyeongtaek for 30 years. Not everyone in the city welcomes the six-digit bonuses making headlines, he indicated, with rising wages among an elite group likely to push up grocery and property prices.

For some, the gap between the bonuses of chip workers and electronics staff hurts all the more because for decades Samsung’s smartphone and home appliances divisions sustained South Korea’s biggest private corporation. Woo Ha-kyung, acting head of the company’s second largest union, said the decision to “gloss over issues” with a mere $4,000 payment to staff not involved in chipmaking undermined their contributions.

Samsung declined to comment.

Workers arrive at Samsung’s Pyeongtaek plant in the early morning in May.Photographer: Tina Hsu/Bloomberg

In an internal [memo](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/samsung-union-votes-in-favor-of-deal-averting-chip-plant-strike) after signing the bonus deal, Samsung Electronics Chief Executive Officer TM Roh said he understood the wage negotiations had left electronics staff feeling “alienated, deprived and perhaps disappointed.” A nearly three-decade veteran of the company, Roh pledged to personally oversee a re-evaluation of business operations. Separately, the company has unveiled a $3.33 billion [fund](https://koreajoongangdaily.joins.com/news/2026-05-27/business/industry/Samsung-announces-5-trillion-won-fund-for-shared-growth-future-talent/2602386 "Korea") to be deployed over the next five years for supplier support, AI talent development and community assistance.

“I will work to ensure your efforts and dedication translate into tangible results and a sense of pride you can truly feel,” Roh wrote.

As the Samsung standoff gripped South Korea, Labor Minister Kim Young-hoon emerged as a central figure. After his last-minute mediations helped stave off a strike, the former union leader held a press conference telling reporters he understood it wasn’t just Samsung staff who felt left behind, but also the general public.

He made another frank admission: When it came to managing the AI bonanza, his government had “no precedent to follow.”

The end of a shift at the Pyeongtaek plant in May.Photographer: Tina Hsu/Bloomberg

Kim had a point. In previous eras, discussions around the world have involved sudden windfalls from oil, minerals or financial surpluses. Those energy and mineral booms have spawned phrases such as “Dutch Disease” and the “Resource Curse” to warn of the negative spillovers from economic jackpots as inflationary pressures spread and wasteful spending ensued.

But this time around, the riches are coming in the tech space and are not led by government-linked entities.

The Pyeongtaek plant is undergoing a major expansion to meet demand.Photographer: Tina Hsu/Bloomberg

Samsung is forecast to generate about 330 trillion won ($221 billion) in operating profit this year, with SK Hynix not far behind. Together, the two firms could contribute more than 100 trillion won in corporate taxes annually. That could exceed the amount the government expected from all companies as recently as April.

The question of how to handle a potential newfound pile of cash and spread the benefits is top of mind for South Korean officials. Kim, the labor minister, has [suggested](https://www.chosun.com/english/national-en/2026/05/28/5MWLPLZMEJB6JDFG5KGLNE4NMA/) a possible social solidarity wage and vowed in a [Facebook](https://www.facebook.com/share/p/1CyrkzkVif/) post that the government “must not simply stand by and watch the gap between workers continue to widen.” A forum initially slated for June 1 to discuss a solidarity wage has been postponed as the details are still being finalized, the Labor Ministry said in a statement.

South Korea is also [reportedly](https://www.hankyung.com/article/2026052126511) looking to allocate some excess tax revenue generated by the chip industry’s super-boom to a “Korean-style sovereign wealth fund.” The government said in a statement that it believed “society would benefit from discussing openly and constructively” how to deal with the AI windfall, and that no decisions had been made regarding the use of future excess tax revenues.

In the Middle East, countries such as Saudi Arabia and the United Arab Emirates have used sovereign wealth funds to channel oil income into global investments and domestic diversification, though these have had limited success in spreading gains broadly across society. Other approaches have focused more on direct redistribution. Alaska’s Permanent Fund pays annual dividends to residents from oil revenues — a rare example of a universal payout that has helped reduce poverty, though the payments are relatively modest.

Waiting for transport after a shift at the plant.Photographer: Tina Hsu/Bloomberg

“Channeling windfalls via the normal fiscal arrangements is more likely to benefit the general public than the introduction of some fund,” said Louis Kuijs, chief Asia-Pacific economist at S&P Global Ratings, who formerly worked at the IMF and World Bank.

As workers in South Korea become increasingly vocal about the windfall from the AI supercycle, Barclays economist Bumki Son said it could evolve into a broader social issue with more people feeling “relatively deprived.”

For Son Ju-ho, a 30-year-old employee of a Samsung partner company, the divide is clear.

“The performance bonuses people talk about — especially on the memory side — don’t really affect us much,” he said, while waiting for a late dinner after a 14-hour day inside the plant. He did see some upsides to the boom, though, as he picked up extra hours: “I was told today we’ll be working 7 a.m. to 9 p.m. shifts for at least the next three to four weeks.”

In a recent [podcast](https://josephnoelwalker.com/peter-costello/ "Click to view webpage."), former Australian Treasurer Peter Costello said that “in many respects, managing prosperity is harder than managing adversity.” He was reflecting on his years of steering Australia’s economy through the start of the China-led mining investment boom that helped push the [budget to surplus](https://treasury.gov.au/publication/2005-04-the-evolution-of-fiscal-policy-in-australia/2005-04-the-evolution-of-fiscal-policy-in-australia "Click to view webpage.") in the late 1990s and early 2000s.

Subsequent governments have swung back to running mainly budget deficits, with crisis management and big-ticket welfare programs eroding the nation’s fiscal position.

Bonuses from Samsung and SK Hynix — which already distributes 10% of operating profit in cash bonuses — are increasingly becoming reference points for wage negotiations across corporate Korea, and beyond. Asia’s other major chip giant Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. [reassured](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/tsmc-s-ceo-pledges-30-plus-incentive-bump-while-ai-profits-soar "TSMC CEO Pledges 30%-Plus Incentive Bump as AI Profits Soar (1)") workers in the wake of Samsung’s decision that a 30% bump in profit-sharing payouts was coming their way, after a chorus of online complaints.

The windfall payments in South Korea could intensify wage growth and services inflation at a time when the central bank is already confronting higher oil prices, rising property values and persistent inflation risks. Chip workers who typically avoid big consumer splurges will likely funnel the cash into savings and investments, according to Bloomberg Economics. That means the first impact is likely to show up in equities and housing.

Lee Se-heePhotographer: Tina Hsu/Bloomberg

In Pyeongtaek, many full-time Samsung employees commute from out of town on company buses. Lee Se-hee, who runs two restaurants near the plant, said the area has benefited from an influx of construction workers tied to Samsung’s expansion. “I’ve heard there are basically no empty studio apartments left,” she said, adding that about 90% of her revenue comes from Samsung employees or those connected.

Even as she welcomes the spoils of the AI boom, the broader debate over technology-driven productivity gains has left Lee uneasy about the future.

“If everything becomes automated, people aren’t needed anymore and the streets will eventually empty out,” she pondered. “What are we supposed to do then?”
