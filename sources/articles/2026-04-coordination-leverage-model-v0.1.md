# The Coordination Leverage Model

## A Management Framework for Agentic AI Transformation

**Janus Digital · AI Office**
**April 2026 · DRAFT v0.1**
**Michael Bruck, Head of AI Office**

---

## 1. The Problem with "AI Native"

Every company now claims to be "AI native" or "AI first." These terms have become marketing shorthand for "we bought some licences and wrote a policy." They describe an aspiration, not a management system. They tell you nothing about how work is organised, how decisions are made, how the organisation scales, or how value compounds.

This document proposes a different foundation. Instead of starting from the technology and asking what it can do, it starts from management science and asks: what organisational problems does agentic AI actually solve, and what management disciplines are required to capture that value systematically?

The answer, grounded in six decades of management theory from Drucker through Grove to the present, is that agentic AI's primary strategic value is not productivity. It is the radical reduction of coordination costs — the overhead that grows superlinearly with organisational complexity and has historically been the binding constraint on scalable growth.

We call this the **Coordination Leverage Model**.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 The Coordination Tax

In 1937, Ronald Coase asked the foundational question of organisational economics: why do firms exist at all? His answer was transaction costs — the overhead of coordinating work through markets exceeds the overhead of coordinating it within a firm. The firm is, in this view, a coordination mechanism that exists because internal coordination is cheaper than external coordination.

But internal coordination is not free. As organisations grow, they accumulate what we term the **coordination tax**: the management layers, reporting structures, status meetings, approval chains, information-routing functions, and context-translation activities that exist not to produce value directly, but to enable value-producing work to happen at scale.

Peter Drucker identified this dynamic in *The Practice of Management* (1954) when he observed that only marketing and innovation produce results; everything else is cost. The "everything else" is largely coordination.

Andy Grove operationalised this insight in *High Output Management* (1983). His core equation — a manager's output equals the output of their organisation plus the output of neighbouring organisations under their influence — is fundamentally a coordination equation. A manager's value is measured by how effectively they reduce friction between productive units. Grove's leverage concept makes this explicit: high-leverage activities are those where a manager's intervention disproportionately increases the output of others.

### 2.2 Why Coordination Costs Scale Superlinearly

Frederick Brooks demonstrated in *The Mythical Man-Month* (1975) that communication paths in a team grow as n(n−1)/2. This is not merely an observation about software teams. It is a general law of organisational complexity: every additional node in an organisation creates communication overhead with every existing node.

Traditional management addresses this through hierarchy — reducing n by creating intermediate nodes (managers) who aggregate and route information. But hierarchy introduces its own costs: latency (information is delayed as it traverses layers), distortion (information is simplified or filtered at each handoff), and rigidity (the structure itself becomes resistant to reconfiguration).

For a company scaling across multiple countries, this compounds. Each new geography adds language, regulatory, timezone, and cultural dimensions to every coordination channel. The coordination tax does not merely grow — it grows multiplicatively with geographical complexity.

### 2.3 The Agentic Inversion

Agentic AI changes the economics of coordination in a way that no previous technology has. Previous automation (ERP, workflow tools, RPA) automated transactions — structured, repeatable actions. Agentic AI automates coordination itself: the interpretation of context, the routing of information, the translation between domains, the synthesis of multiple inputs into coherent outputs, and the maintenance of institutional context across interactions.

This is the inversion. Rather than building management layers to handle coordination overhead, organisations can deploy agents to perform the coordination function directly. The firm's cost structure shifts: the coordination tax is no longer proportional to headcount and geography; it becomes proportional to the capability and orchestration of the agent infrastructure.

> *The question is no longer "how many managers do we need to coordinate this work?" but "what coordination functions can agents perform, and what must remain human?"*

## 3. The Three-Layer Model

Agentic AI transformation occurs across three distinct organisational layers. Each layer has different unit economics, different management disciplines, and different failure modes. Critically, each layer produces the structured data that enables the next — making them sequentially dependent but cumulatively compounding.

| Layer | Unit of Analysis | Management Discipline | Theoretical Basis | AI Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Individual** | One person's output | Task design & leverage | Drucker, Grove | Force multiplier |
| **2. Department** | Team information topology | Process architecture | Grove, Nonaka | Connective tissue |
| **3. Organisation** | Company-wide knowledge | Structural design | Chandler, Coase | Coordination layer |

### 3.1 Layer 1 — Individual Leverage

**Unit of analysis:** A single knowledge worker's output.

This is Drucker's original knowledge worker productivity problem, now solvable at scale. AI at this layer acts as a force multiplier on existing tasks: drafting, research, analysis, synthesis, code generation, translation. The individual produces more output per unit of time.

**The management discipline is task design.** Grove's leverage concept applies directly: a high-leverage activity amplified by AI produces disproportionate output. But AI also makes people faster at low-leverage activities. Without deliberate task design, individuals will optimise for throughput on the wrong work. The manager's role at this layer is not to monitor output volume but to ensure that AI-amplified effort is directed at the highest-leverage activities.

*Key risk: Productivity theatre — people producing more of the wrong things, faster. Drucker's distinction between efficiency (doing things right) and effectiveness (doing the right things) has never been more operationally critical.*

**Output to next layer:** Structured, machine-readable work products (not PDFs and emails) that can be ingested by department-level systems.

### 3.2 Layer 2 — Department Architecture

**Unit of analysis:** The information topology of a team or department.

At this layer, AI is no longer a tool attached to an individual. It is the connective tissue of the department — the pipelines, workflows, and agentic processes that move information between people, between systems, and between decisions. The standup pipeline (Fireflies → transcript → Linear → Notion), the Skills Library, the structured data hygiene practices — these are Layer 2 artefacts.

**The management discipline is process architecture.** This is Grove's "process-oriented" management, but with a critical difference: the process is not merely documented and enforced by humans — it is enacted by agents. The connective tissue that traditionally required coordinators, project managers, and administrative staff is replaced by agentic pipelines that maintain context, route information, and surface exceptions for human judgment.

Nonaka and Takeuchi's knowledge creation spiral (1995) becomes operationally relevant here. Their SECI model — Socialisation, Externalisation, Combination, Internalisation — describes how tacit knowledge becomes explicit and institutional. Agentic workflows at Layer 2 accelerate the Externalisation and Combination phases by capturing, structuring, and connecting knowledge that would otherwise remain in individual heads or unstructured channels.

*Key risk: Over-engineering. Not every process benefits from agentic automation. The prototype-to-production gap is real: the cognitive load of managing multiple agents simultaneously is itself a coordination cost. Process architecture must be selective — automate the high-frequency, high-context coordination tasks first.*

**Output to next layer:** Clean, structured, continuously updated departmental data that feeds the company-wide knowledge base.

### 3.3 Layer 3 — Organisational Scale

**Unit of analysis:** The company's institutional knowledge and coordination capacity.

This is where Chandler's "structure follows strategy" meets Coase's transaction cost theory. The traditional answer to geographical scaling is the multidivisional form — semi-autonomous country or regional units with their own management layers, connected to headquarters through reporting lines and periodic reviews. This structure exists because the coordination cost of tight integration across geographies historically exceeded the cost of local duplication.

Agentic AI at Layer 3 changes this equation. When agents can maintain institutional context, translate between regulatory frameworks, aggregate cross-market intelligence, and route decisions to the appropriate human authority regardless of timezone — the rationale for heavy local management structures weakens. The organisation can operate with a thinner human layer in each geography, focused on the judgment-intensive work that requires local presence: relationship building, regulatory navigation, cultural adaptation, and strategic market positioning.

**The management discipline is structural design.** The question is not "how do we replicate our structure in each new country?" but "what is the minimum viable human presence per geography, given that the coordination layer is handled by agents?"

*Key risk: Premature abstraction. The world model must be built from real, clean, structured data — not from aspirational data architectures. Layer 3 is only as good as the data produced by Layer 2, which is only as good as the structured outputs from Layer 1. Attempting to build the organisational layer before the department layer is operational is a common and expensive failure mode.*

## 4. The Compounding Mechanism

### 4.1 Structured Data as Institutional Capital

The three layers are not merely sequential. They are compounding. Each layer produces structured data that increases the capability of the layer above it. This compounding dynamic is the primary strategic advantage of the Coordination Leverage Model over ad hoc AI deployment.

At Layer 1, individual work products become machine-readable inputs. At Layer 2, these inputs are connected into departmental knowledge systems with lineage, context, and decision history. At Layer 3, departmental knowledge systems feed a company-wide institutional model that agents can query, reason over, and act upon.

This is not a data warehouse. It is what we have termed the "world model of the company" — a continuously updated, structured representation of the organisation's state, decisions, capabilities, and institutional knowledge. Its value compounds over time: every interaction, every decision, every structured output makes the model richer and the agents more capable.

> *Structured data hygiene is not an IT concern. It is the capital investment that funds organisational scaling. Every unstructured document, every decision made in email rather than a system of record, every piece of institutional knowledge that lives only in someone's head is a missed compounding opportunity.*

### 4.2 The Flywheel

The compounding dynamic creates a flywheel:

| Step | Dynamic |
|---|---|
| 1 | Individuals produce structured outputs (Layer 1) |
| 2 | Agentic pipelines connect and contextualise those outputs (Layer 2) |
| 3 | The institutional model becomes richer and more capable (Layer 3) |
| 4 | Richer institutional context enables better individual outputs (back to Layer 1) |
| 5 | Coordination costs decrease as a proportion of total activity with each cycle |

This is the mechanism by which "AI Native" becomes operationally meaningful: not as a label, but as a measurable, compounding reduction in coordination costs relative to value-producing activity.

## 5. Management Disciplines Per Layer

Each layer requires a distinct management focus. Applying the wrong discipline at the wrong layer is a common failure mode — and one that "AI Native" branding actively obscures by treating everything as a single transformation.

| Layer | Manager's Question | Grove Analogy | Failure Mode | Success Metric |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Individual** | Is this person doing high-leverage work? | Task-relevant maturity → autonomy calibration | Productivity theatre | Output quality per decision |
| **Department** | Is information flowing without human bottlenecks? | Process-oriented management with agents | Over-engineering, brittle pipelines | Cycle time, data completeness |
| **Organisation** | Can we enter a new market without replicating the coordination layer? | Mission-oriented management from day one | Premature abstraction, empty world model | Revenue per employee, time to market entry |

### 5.1 Grove's Task-Relevant Maturity — Applied to Agents

Grove's concept of task-relevant maturity (TRM) — the idea that the appropriate level of managerial involvement depends on the subordinate's competence for the specific task — maps directly onto agent autonomy design. An agent handling a well-defined, low-risk pipeline (e.g., transcript → structured summary → system of record update) has high task-relevant maturity and should operate with minimal human oversight. An agent making decisions with strategic or financial implications has low TRM for that task and requires tight human-in-the-loop controls.

This is more rigorous than the vague "human in the loop" mantra that most AI governance frameworks rely on. TRM provides a principled basis for calibrating agent autonomy: not all decisions need human oversight, and not all automation is safe to run unattended. The calibration depends on the task, not the technology.

### 5.2 Drucker's Effectiveness Principle — The Layer 1 Gate

Drucker's insight that effectiveness (doing the right things) is more important than efficiency (doing things right) functions as the gating discipline for Layer 1. Before amplifying any individual's work with AI, the first question must be: should this work be done at all? AI makes it dangerously easy to produce polished outputs for tasks that should not exist.

This has direct implications for role design. In an AI-augmented organisation, the most valuable individual contributors are not those who produce the most output, but those who exercise the best judgment about what to produce. The management task is to create the conditions — clear priorities, clean systems of record, rapid feedback loops — that enable good judgment.

### 6.2 The AI Office as Layer 2 Prototype

The AI Office team is deliberately operating as the prototype for Layer 2 transformation. The daily standup pipeline, the skills library, and the structured Linear/Notion data architecture are not just operational conveniences — they are the proof that agentic workflows can replace coordination overhead within a department.

This is the case study that validates the model before it is applied to other departments. The sequence is deliberate: build it for ourselves, prove the compounding dynamic, document the patterns (skills library), then replicate to Technology (via Euclid's team), Finance (via AIP-1), and subsequently to country operations.

### 6.3 Scaling Implications

For Janus Digital's multi-country expansion, the Coordination Leverage Model predicts that the traditional scaling curve — each new market requires 6–12 months of organisational build-out before becoming productive — can be significantly compressed. If Layer 2 department architectures are portable (via the skills library and standardised agentic pipelines), and Layer 3 institutional context travels with the agents (via the world model), then new market entry requires only the minimum viable human presence: relationship builders, regulatory navigators, and local market strategists.

The coordination layer — reporting, compliance monitoring, knowledge sharing, onboarding, operational playbooks — arrives with the infrastructure, not with the people.

## 7. Architectural Design Principles

The following principles govern system design decisions across all three layers. They are derived from the theoretical foundations and operational experience documented in this framework.

| # | Principle | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Layer Sequence Is Not Optional** | Layer 1 feeds Layer 2 feeds Layer 3. Attempting to build higher layers before lower layers are producing structured data is premature abstraction. Build bottom-up, architect top-down. |
| 2 | **Automate Coordination, Not Judgment** | Agents should handle information routing, context maintenance, status tracking, and structured data management. Humans should handle strategy, relationships, exception handling, and decisions with material consequences. |
| 3 | **Structured Data Is Capital** | Every workflow design decision should ask: does this produce structured, machine-readable data that compounds? If the output is a PDF, an email, or an unstructured Slack message, the compounding value is lost. |
| 4 | **Task-Relevant Maturity Governs Autonomy** | Agent autonomy is calibrated per task, not per agent. The same agent may operate autonomously for routine coordination and require human approval for consequential decisions. |
| 5 | **Systems of Record Over Communication Channels** | Decisions, status changes, and institutional knowledge must live in queryable systems of record (Linear, Notion), not in transient communication channels (Slack, email). Communication announces; systems of record persist. |
| 6 | **Portability Over Optimisation** | Department-level agentic architectures must be designed for replication across geographies. Optimise for portability and standardisation before optimising for local performance. |
| 7 | **The Drucker Gate** | Before amplifying any activity with AI, ask: should this activity exist at all? Efficiency applied to unnecessary work is waste at scale. |

## 8. The Grove Test: How to Know It's Working

Grove insisted that management must be measured by output, not activity. The following indicators, mapped to each layer, provide a diagnostic for whether the Coordination Leverage Model is producing real value or merely generating the appearance of transformation.

### 8.1 Layer 1 Indicators

Individuals are spending more time on high-leverage activities and less on context-gathering, status reporting, and information routing. The proportion of working time spent on judgment-intensive tasks (strategy, analysis, client engagement, creative problem-solving) increases relative to administrative and coordinative tasks. This is measurable through time allocation studies and output audits.

### 8.2 Layer 2 Indicators

Department cycle times for recurring processes decrease without quality degradation. Information reaches the right person at the right time without human routing. The ratio of people producing direct value to people coordinating value production shifts in favour of production. Systems of record are current, complete, and queryable — not stale or manually maintained.

### 8.3 Layer 3 Indicators

New market entry timelines compress. The cost of adding a geography grows sublinearly rather than linearly or superlinearly. Cross-market knowledge sharing happens through systems, not through meetings. Revenue per employee increases as the organisation scales — Bonaventure's north-star metric — because the coordination tax is not scaling with headcount.

### 8.4 The Anti-Indicators

The model is failing if: people are producing more outputs but the outputs are not connected to systems of record; department-level pipelines exist but break frequently and require constant manual intervention; the organisation is deploying AI tools without a clear theory of which layer they serve; or the coordination tax is growing alongside the AI investment rather than being displaced by it.

## 9. From Framework to Practice

The Coordination Leverage Model provides Janus Digital with an intellectually grounded, operationally specific alternative to "AI Native" branding. It answers three questions that the branding does not:

**What problem are we solving?** The coordination tax that scales superlinearly with organisational complexity and geographical expansion.

**How does AI solve it?** By performing the coordination function itself — information routing, context maintenance, cross-domain translation — at a cost that does not scale with headcount or geography.

**How do we manage the transformation?** Through three distinct management disciplines applied at three distinct organisational layers, sequenced bottom-up, with structured data hygiene as the compounding mechanism that connects them.

The AI Office exists to build this. The current prototype — Layer 2 within the AI Office itself — is the proof of concept. The roadmap is Layer 2 replication across departments, then Layer 3 activation as the structured data from those departments feeds the institutional world model.

The destination is an organisation where coordination costs are a diminishing fraction of total activity, where new geographies can be entered with minimal structural overhead, and where the institutional knowledge of the entire company is available to every person and every agent, structured, current, and actionable.

That is what "AI Native" should mean.
