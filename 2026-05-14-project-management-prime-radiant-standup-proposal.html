How the standup works

One brain for the team. Team-only for now.

Four things happen — most of which you do not have to do. Same pattern Marketing is rolling out with, adapted to the workflow Lysander walked us through.

  • 1 Project Management team vault stood up (GitHub-backed).
    We create a private GitHub repo for the team under the Janusd-com organisation. Each of you gets a local clone on your machine that stays in sync automatically — you never touch GitHub directly, the tooling handles it. This becomes the canonical Project Management knowledge store, replacing the ad-hoc "each Project Manager has their own files" pattern Lysander flagged.
  • 2 Vault is initialised against your 28-phase workflow.
    The folder structure mirrors how you actually work (see next slide). The first templates inside it come from the workflow itself: project charter, formal handoff minutes, project management plan, plan review minutes, BIP kickoff brief, verification plan, training matrix. Each phase has its expected inputs / outputs as starter docs. This is the "running start" content — you do not start from a blank page.
  • 3 Tooling installed on your machines (single session, ~60–90 minutes).
    Six things, most of them you already have or will use seamlessly:
    • Cowork — your conversation with the brain.
    • Obsidian + GitHub plugin — your view into the vault; the GitHub plugin handles the sync silently so you only see the file structure.
    • Web Clipper — one-click article capture into the inbox.
    • Fireflies connector — meetings flow into the brain automatically.
    • Monday.com — system of record for tasks, projects, action tracking. Already in use across Janus; the brain reads from it.
    • Google Drive (likely) — for document storage where appropriate. We'll confirm scope of Drive usage with you during the install.
  • 4 First content backfill — one live project.
    We pick one of your current projects together and ingest its existing materials — meeting transcripts, the project charter if one exists, RAID, status updates, decisions made along the way. Watching the brain ingest a real Project Management workload is where it earns the trust to be used on the next one.
Two things we'll cover in a separate conversation
Personal vaults are not part of this rollout. Earlier framings (including the company-wide intro deck) showed personal vaults alongside team vaults. Jehad and I decided this morning to shelve them — the rules for what flows from a personal vault into the team vault are more complicated than we want to solve right now. We'll come back to personal vaults once the team brains have proved out. Federation between department brains (the AIO × Project Management shared workspace) also needs a separate conversation — our recent move to GitHub as the canonical substrate means the previous mesh-subfolder pattern (which assumed Google Shared Drive) has to be re-thought. Not a blocker for your standup; we figure it out together once the team brain is live.

The folder structure — react and refine

Built around your workflow, not imposed on top of it.

Below is the proposed structure. The aim is that you push back, refine, or replace before we lock it. Once locked, the vault can be initialised in under an hour.

Proposed structure
project-management/ ├── products/ # durable across projects │ └── <product>/ │ ├── overview.md │ ├── roadmap.md │ └── decisions/ ├── projects/ # episodic; phase-shaped │ └── <project>/ │ ├── charter.md # phase 2 │ ├── scope.md # phase 3 loop output │ ├── handoff/ # phase 4 minutes │ ├── planning/ # phases 5–8 │ ├── parallel-tracks/# phase 6 │ │ ├── construction/ │ │ ├── hardware/ │ │ └── platform/ │ ├── execution/ # phases 9–17 │ ├── digital-delivery/# phase 17 │ ├── business-delivery/# phase 20 │ ├── uat/ # phases 23–24 │ ├── training/ # phase 25 │ ├── trial-run/ # phases 26–27 │ ├── verification/ # phase 28 │ ├── decisions/ # project-level │ ├── raid/ │ └── meetings/ ├── stakeholders/ # cross-project people ├── processes/ # your playbooks ├── templates/ # versioned ├── retros/ # lessons across projects └── decisions/ # function-level
Why this shape
  • 1 Products are durable. Projects are episodic.
    A product persists across many projects; a project finishes. Putting products at the top level keeps long-lived knowledge findable. Projects link up into products as they touch them.
  • 2 Project folders mirror your 28-phase workflow.
    Each project sub-folder maps directly to a phase Lysander walked us through. When a Project Manager opens a new project folder, the structure tells them what comes next; the templates inside it tell them how.
  • 3 Decisions live at three levels.
    Function-level (top-level decisions/: "we always do X across all projects"); product-level (durable to that product); project-level (specific to one project). Each decision is dated, has a why, and links to what it affects. The ECO discipline Michael mentioned — versioned, traceable.
  • 4 Templates versioned alongside the work.
    No more "version 1.0 in this PM's folder, version 2.0 in that one." Git versioning handles it; the brain knows which is current. Templates evolve as you discover better ways to do things — every change has a decision behind it.
Open questions for the alignment conversation
Where do portfolios sit (above products)? Where do programmes live (between products and projects, or as a flat tag)? Where does vendor / third-party / supplier content live? Are there phases where the current naming doesn't match what your team actually says? — Lysander, you'll have the strongest read here.

Each of you commits to using the brain at least once a day for the first two weeks — even when it would have been faster to just ask a colleague. The brain compounds in direct proportion to that discipline. Five working days of forced use; from week two it starts repaying you. That's the only behavioural change we are asking for up front.
