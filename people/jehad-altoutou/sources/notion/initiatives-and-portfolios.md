## What Linear Initiatives actually are
An Initiative is a workspace-level, manually curated list of projects plus a long-form doc. The data model is intentionally thin: a name, an owner, a status (Planned / Active / Completed), a target date, a description, a resources block, and a list of attached projects. There is no "private initiative" — everything is workspace-visible (guests excluded). Each project can be attached to one or more initiatives.
The value isn't in the object itself, it's in three derived behaviors layered on top:
**Health rollup.** The initiative's health is whatever its most recent Initiative Update said (On track / At risk / Off track). The "Active Projects" column rolls up the most recent Project Updates of the attached projects, color-coded the same way, with grey for "no recent update." This is the leadership glance-view: one screen, a row per goal, RAG status visible without opening anything.
**Initiative graph.** Per-project burn-down of completed issues over time, overlaid in one chart. Useful for spotting which workstreams are actually moving vs. stalled.
**Update cadence with Slack as the carrier.** This is the part most relevant to your "AI as management assistant" framing. Admins enable reminders (daily / weekly / biweekly, specific day & time), and Linear nudges the owner/lead in their local timezone, then re-nudges +1 working day, +2 working days. Updates have a structured shape (health indicator + rich text + auto-injected delta — target date moves, lead changes, \>2% progress changes are appended automatically). Updates post into a designated Slack channel (e.g. `#initiative-updates`, `#project-updates`), and Slack thread comments sync bidirectionally back into Linear. There's also a "stale" state for projects that miss a cycle — health icon dashes, then turns grey.
## Sub-initiatives: the catch
Sub-initiatives let you nest up to five levels deep, with parent initiatives auto-rolling up child projects. **They're Enterprise-tier only.** Before we plan around a hierarchy, we need to confirm Janus's Linear plan — if you're on Standard or Business, you get a flat list of initiatives, full stop. That meaningfully changes the structural design conversation from the standup.
## Where this slots into Janus
The discovery I want to flag: AI Registry and AI Projects are currently modeled as Projects (with team-level umbrella Project objects under each Team). That's actually a structural mismatch — they're long-running, multi-workstream umbrellas with no real "done" date. Those are exactly what Initiatives were built for. The current shape works because you didn't have a higher tier; with Initiatives turned on, the natural redraw is:
- **Initiatives** = AI Registry buildout, AI Office productionization, plus any cross-team goal (e.g. "Finance AI enablement," "Standup-to-decision automation")
- **Projects** = bounded efforts under those initiatives ("Sandbox Monday.com as SOR," "Linear↔Notion bridge cleanup," each individual tool evaluation past Gate 2)
- **Issues** = the work itself (where AIR-\* and AIP-\* live today)
This also reframes the Notion proposal from #80. The standup pitched Notion holding "departmental themes / objectives / initiatives" with Linear holding "projects and tasks." But Linear Initiatives covers the objective/initiative slot natively, with health, updates, and Slack rhythm built-in. So the cleaner split is probably: **Linear owns the operational hierarchy from initiative down**, **Notion narrows to the narrative layer above that** (departmental charters, OKR rationale, decision logs, playbooks) — which both reduces what Jehad would have to mirror in Monday and removes the drift problem entirely. That's a more aggressive simplification than what was on the table at standup; worth deciding explicitly.
## On the "AI as management assistant" thesis
Initiatives give an AI assistant something it doesn't have today: a stable, named, leadership-vocabulary set of objects to reason about. Without them, asking "how is X going" forces the AI to crawl issue lists and infer. With them, the AI can answer at the right altitude — read the latest Initiative Update, the rolled-up project healths, the Slack thread on the most recent At Risk update — and produce the kind of answer you'd want from a chief of staff. Specifically, three handles open up:
The reminder cadence becomes an AI drafting target — pre-populate the next update from issue activity + the Slack thread since the last one, owner just reviews.
The RAG signal becomes summarizable — "anything new turning yellow this week?" is now a trivial query.
The Slack-side comments become structured input — bidirectional sync means the AI can read the thread that led to a status change without scraping.
## Risks / gotchas before we touch anything
Confirm the plan tier (Enterprise gates sub-initiatives). Initiatives are workspace-public — no private strategic goals, which matters if anything is sensitive. Both AIR and AIP need to stay public teams for project updates to land in the workspace-default Slack channel. And the obvious one: don't pre-populate a deep tree before you have actual cadence — flat list of 3–5 real initiatives, run two update cycles, then decide if hierarchy is needed.

## The concept map (Linear → Monday)
Monday actually offers **two separate features** that together cover Linear's initiative concept, plus a third tangentially related one:
**Portfolio Management.** Monday's hierarchy is `Portfolio → Program → Project → Task`. Each Project has a built-in **Project Health** field with `On Track / At Risk / Off Track` (the same RAG semantics as Linear). The Portfolio view rolls up child-project health into a card-based "Portfolio Health Snapshot" — same purpose as Linear's Active Projects column. There's also an Enterprise-only **Portfolio Risk Insights** that runs daily AI risk analysis across the projects.
**Goals & OKRs.** A separate feature, focused on objective/key-result/initiative cascade. Note that Monday uses "initiative" in the OKR-vocabulary sense — meaning *tactics under a key result* — which is different from Linear's usage where Initiative is the top-level container. So if you say "initiative" inside Monday, expect a vocabulary collision.
**Workdocs.** The narrative/document layer that attaches to boards and items. This is the closest thing to Linear's Initiative description doc.
So the natural Monday mapping for what we sketched in Linear is:
- Linear Initiative → Monday **Project** inside a **Portfolio**, with Workdoc attached
- Linear Project → Monday **sub-project** (a child Project, since Monday allows Project nesting in the Portfolio model)
- Linear Issue → Monday **Item / Task**
- Linear Initiative Update → Monday Project's **updates feed** (rich text comments) + the Project Health field as the RAG indicator
- Linear's weekly reminder + Slack post → Monday **automation recipe** ("every Wednesday at 9am, notify owner to post update; cross-post to Slack channel #project-updates")
## The real gaps vs. Linear
Three things Linear gives you in 30 seconds that Monday makes you build:
**The structured Initiative Update flow.** Linear has a dedicated "Update" object with a health indicator, rich text body, and auto-injected delta (target date moved, lead changed, \>2% progress shift). In Monday, the closest match is a Status column for RAG plus a free-text update. You can replicate the structure with a Monday Form ("Post update: health \[On/At/Off\], what changed, blockers, next") that creates a sub-item or appends to the project's updates feed — but it's a config job, not a checkbox.
**Cadence reminders that escalate.** Linear nudges +1 day, +2 days when an update is overdue. Monday's automation builder can replicate this (it's just two more recipes: "if no update posted by Thursday, notify owner"; "if still no update by Friday, notify owner+lead") but you have to build them.
**Plan tiering.** Monday's Portfolio Management is a paid add-on, not bundled with standard Work Management. Pricing varies, but it adds meaningfully on top of the per-seat Pro tier (\~\$19/user/month). The Enterprise-only AI Risk Insights would be the equivalent of having a chief-of-staff agent watching the boards — currently behind Enterprise pricing.
## Do you have to build it yourself?
**No, you don't have to** — Portfolio Management gives you the primitives (hierarchy, RAG, rollup) out of the box. What you'd be "building" is the *update rhythm*: forms, automations, Slack recipes, and a discipline-enforcing template. That's 1-2 days of configuration, not a real engineering project.
**You could fake it without Portfolio**, on a cheaper tier, using:
- One "Initiatives" board (each row = one initiative, with a Status column for RAG, an owner column, target date, and a Workdoc subitem)
- One "Projects" board with a Connect Boards column linking each project to its parent initiative
- Mirror columns from Projects → Initiatives to roll up health
- Automation recipes for cadence + Slack posts
This works and avoids paying for Portfolio. The downside is you maintain the rollup logic — when Monday changes its mirror-column behavior, you fix it. With Portfolio you offload that to Monday.
